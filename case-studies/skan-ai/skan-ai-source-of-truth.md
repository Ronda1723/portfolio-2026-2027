### What is Skan AI?

Skan AI is a Menlo Park-based enterprise software company building **process intelligence and agentic AI** for the modern enterprise. Their core technology is a Large Process Model (LPM) — a multimodal vision-language model trained on hundreds of enterprise process screenshots and event-level action descriptors. The LPM enables AI agents to understand, replicate, and optimize complex enterprise workflows by reasoning over event flows, recognizing process states, and executing actions within containerized environments.

In plain English: Skan teaches AI agents to do enterprise work by watching how humans do it, then deploys those agents with built-in self-correction, expert-in-the-loop escalation, and continuous KPI-driven optimization.

The technical depth of what Skan does is significant. The audience for their pitch decks — investors, enterprise buyers, partners — needs to understand it without being overwhelmed by it. **That's where Samita came in.**

### Samita's role in one sentence

She translated dense, technical AI/ML architecture into clean, layered, editable visual narratives that founders could confidently present in front of investors at firms like Insight Partners.

### Why this project matters for Samita's portfolio

1. **Real client, real money, real stakes.** Not a class project. Not a hypothetical brief. A founder trusted her to design slides that would be presented in pitches affecting the company's funding.
2. **Real-world outcome.** The Insight Partners deck (engagement 2) was actually used in investor meetings. The work directly supported Skan's investor relations.
3. **Translating complexity into clarity.** This is the core skill of any good designer working on AI products. She took 49+ technical concepts in a single architecture diagram and made them legible for non-technical decision-makers.
4. **Working with founders, not committees.** Direct WhatsApp/Zoom communication with the founder, fast iteration cycles, no bureaucracy. This is how early-stage AI startups operate — and it's the environment she's targeting in her job search.
5. **The first paid AI work in her portfolio.** Everything else in her portfolio is academic or self-directed. Skan AI is the proof point that her skills translate to professional, paid AI work.

## Part 2: The Two Engagements

### Engagement 1: Pitch Deck Redesign (with Avinash Misra, Founder)

**Timeline:** August 25 – September 8, 2025

**Origin:** Rajesh, a mutual contact, introduced Samita to Avinash. Their first conversation was a 15-minute Zoom on August 25. Avinash needed presentation help and asked about pricing. Samita quoted her standard $30–50/hr range and offered flexibility given the warm intro. Avinash's response set the tone: *"It would be a pleasure to reward you handsomely. My only ask is the highest quality of work."*

**NDA:** Signed within 48 hours of first contact.

**Scope:** Comprehensive redesign of an existing 50+ slide pitch deck. The work covered visual hierarchy, iconography, layouts, content restructuring, color theme transitions, chart design, and slide-level rebuilds for the most critical narrative beats.

**Specific slide work documented:**
- **Slides 11–12:** Originally split across multiple slides; Samita combined per founder feedback
- **Slides 7, 8, 9:** Combined into one slide with VentureBeat and Wall Street Journal screenshots integrated
- **Slide 14:** Tech stack visualization with custom iconography
- **Slide 16:** Stack/layering visualization with bidirectional arrow logic between Human Systems, Work Surface, and Skan
- **Slide 17:** Layered visual designed to support animation (each layer separable for the founder to animate individually)
- **Slide 30:** ACV growth chart — Samita pushed back on showing a 110M figure to-scale because it would make historical numbers visually disappear; suggested showing to-scale only through 2025 instead. (This is the kind of design judgment that earns trust.)
- **Slide 48 — "The Challenge for Agentic AI":** Rebuilt twice. First with structure and iconography, then with added depth, layering, and visual stylization. Ended in approval: *"Thanks. This is fine."*
- **Slide 60:** "The missing layer for the AI native enterprise" — designed with iconography, layout exploration, custom approach
- **Slide 8:** Customer trust slide — *"Our customers are brands the world trusts"*
- **Maturity model slide (the photo I have):** A 5-stage horizontal flow showing how Skan customers progress from Process Excellence → Automation → Workforce Productivity → Controls Monitoring → Agentic AI

**Theme exploration:** Initial direction was a black background, which Avinash rejected: *"This black background is a little bit too much. It just doesn't work if every slide is black."* Samita pivoted, explored 2-3 themes (white, light, alternating), and landed on a clean white theme with depth and layering that became the deck's visual language going forward.

**Working style discoveries:**
- Avinash preferred fewer, denser slides over many simpler ones (*"We don't have that much time to toggle so many slides for the audience"*)
- He wanted everything editable — never grouped or flattened (*"Please never join images together so that I can't edit them"*)
- Tight feedback loops: *"Just ping me when you have adjusted one background so I can tell you whether it works or not"*
- Direct, demanding, but fair — when Samita's first version of slide 14 wasn't differentiated enough, he said so plainly. When she got it right on slide 16, he said so plainly.

**Key invoice from this engagement (Invoice #001 — sent September 2, 2025):**
> *"In the meanwhile, could you send me a bill for the work done till now?"*

**Outcome:** The deck was used by Avinash for a presentation on September 2. Followup work continued through September 8.

### The technical conversion work (Sep 27, 2025) — the hardest deliverable

Between the two engagements, on September 27, Avinash sent a message from a flight: *"some work for you - these are 4 svg diagrams, can you convert them into powerpoint slides. professional looking with slick icons, connectors. I should be able to edit the text and move around the boxes."*

This was the most technically demanding task in the entire engagement. Samita had to convert four extremely dense Draw.io SVG architecture diagrams into editable widescreen PowerPoint slides that the founder could continue to refine.

**The diagrams:**

1. **SkanOverall_Agentic_Architecture.svg** — The full Skan agentic architecture, containing 49+ distinct technical concepts including: Data Ingestion, Preprocessing, Action Processing using Skan LPM, Process State Recognition, Screen Comprehension, User Action Recognition, Process Instance Understanding, Step-by-Step Case Understanding, Case Trajectory Alignment, Decision Point Mapping, Trajectory Variance Analysis, Global Process Map Construction, Agent Initialization, Action Execution Engine, Screen State Verification, Expected vs Actual State Evaluation, Path Confirmation, Human Expert in the Loop, Guided Agentic Execution, Skan LPM continual learning, Bridging Process Abstraction, Agent Playbook Generation, Business Document Repositories, Evaluator LLM as a Judge, KPI driven Automation Optimization, Continuous Optimization, Exception Handling — and more.

2. **SKAN_LAM.svg** — Skan's foundational Large Action/Process Model architecture: Multimodal VLM, Anonymized Screenshots, Event Level Action Descriptors, Self-Supervised Domain Adaptive Pretraining (DAPT), Action-Outcome Alignment, Process-Aware Reasoning Pretraining via GRPO, the LAM/LPM itself.

3. **Skan_LAM_enterpriseSpecificGrounding.svg** — Persona-specific fine-tuning architecture: Visual Case Streams, Skan Discovered Event Metadata, Enterprise-defined Success Metrics, Modality-specialized Enterprise LAM, Conditional Optimization over Business KPIs.

4. (Plus a fourth diagram referenced in the message)

**The critical rename:** *"one imp thing where it everywhere it says large action model, it should be replaced by large process model. LPM or Large Process Model, instead of LAM or Large action model."*

This wasn't just a find-and-replace task. Samita had to maintain the integrity of every technical term, every connector relationship, and every visual hierarchy across these diagrams while making them editable, professional, and slick. The final deliverable (`09-27-2025 - Skan AI.pptx`) was 5 widescreen (13.33" × 7.5") slides with 29, 23, and 30+ shapes per slide — every element editable, every relationship preserved, every "LAM" updated to "LPM."

This is the work that proves the case study's central claim: Samita can take dense enterprise AI architecture and make it visually legible without losing technical accuracy.

## Part 4: What Samita Actually Did (The Skills)

### 1. Translation of technical complexity into visual clarity

Skan's technology touches on multimodal VLMs, GRPO-based reasoning pretraining, self-supervised DAPT, agentic execution loops, LLM-as-judge evaluation, KPI-driven optimization, and exception handling modules. Samita doesn't need to build any of this — but she has to make it understandable in 30 seconds to investors who need to grasp it without feeling overwhelmed.

This is the hardest skill in AI product design and the most valuable: **making complex systems legible to non-technical decision-makers without dumbing them down.**

### 2. Visual storytelling at the slide-narrative level

Each slide had to do specific narrative work in the deck. Samita understood (or quickly learned) what each slide needed to communicate, then designed the visual hierarchy to support that narrative beat. The maturity model slide had to feel like progression. The architecture diagrams had to feel like a coherent system. The customer trust slide had to feel like authority.

### 3. Iconography, layout, and depth

Avinash's repeated feedback was: more depth, more layering, more iconography, more visual stylization. Samita developed a custom visual language for the deck that balanced executive polish with technical detail.

### 4. Editability discipline

Every slide had to be fully editable by the founder. No flattened images. No grouped objects. Everything separable for animation. Everything modifiable in PowerPoint. This is a discipline that sounds boring but matters enormously when a founder is iterating live before a pitch.

### 5. Pushback and design judgment

The ACV chart conversation is the best example. Samita didn't just execute — she flagged a problem (showing 110M to-scale would visually erase the historical numbers) and proposed an alternative (only show to-scale through 2025). This is the design judgment that turns a contractor into a trusted collaborator.

### 6. Speed and reliability

Tight turnarounds. The Insight Partners deck went from raw materials to 41 polished slides in 48 hours. The slide 48 rebuild went from request to draft in under an hour. Founders trust designers who deliver fast and well.

### 7. Working with founders directly

WhatsApp messages, Zoom calls, async feedback, terse direction. Samita matched the founder's working style: brief, fast, action-oriented. No design theater, no decks-about-decks, no process for the sake of process. Just work.

She is not a "deck designer." Framing this case study as "I made PowerPoint slides" sells it short and undersells her actual skill.

This wasn't a months-long design system project. It was a series of high-stakes, fast-turnaround design tasks where each slide mattered. The case study's strength is the **density** of the work and the **realness** of the outcome — not the duration or scope.
